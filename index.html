Welcome to the Orrery: Near-Earth Objects Viewer!

Created by NASA Space Apps Challenge team: Bil Gates

Space is vast, but it’s filled with fascinating objects—many of them closer to Earth than

you might think. With over 6,000 satellites in orbit and countless comets, asteroids, and

meteors, new discoveries are happening all the time. Our goal is to make tracking and

learning about these Near-Earth Objects (NEOs) as simple as possible

What You Can Do Here:

- Explore detailed data on Near-Earth Objects

- Track the real-time movement of comets, meteors, and satellites

- Access the detailed information about satellite’s navigation, mission and history

NEOs INFO

NEOs(asteroids)

Asteroids are minor planets that orbit within the inner Solar System and are classified into

C-type (carbonaceous), M-type (metallic), or S-type (silicaceous) based on their

composition. They vary in size, from small rubble piles to Ceres, a dwarf planet nearly 1000

km in diameter. Most asteroids are found in the main asteroid belt between Mars and

Jupiter. Missions like NASA's NEAR Shoemaker, JAXA’s Hayabusa, and OSIRIS-REx have

studied asteroids up close, with samples returned to Earth. Near-Earth asteroids pose a

potential threat, as seen with the Chicxulub impact, but efforts like the 2022 DART mission

have explored asteroid deflection techniques.

Satellites

A satellite is a spacecraft placed in orbit around a celestial body, serving purposes like

communication, navigation, weather monitoring, scientific research, and military uses.

Most satellites generate electricity via solar panels, use transponders for communication,

and are lightweight to reduce launch costs. Launched by rockets, they primarily orbit in

low Earth or geostationary orbits. Satellites also gather data for Earth observation and

space exploration. The first artificial satellite, Sputnik 1, was launched in 1957, and by 2022,

there were 6,718 operational satellites in Earth's orbit.
